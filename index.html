Avrie Allen is an interdisciplinary designer.

She recently received an MFA in Graphic Design from Rhode Island School of Design (RISD), and works across graphic design, website development, book design, physical installation, publishing, and writing. She has taught a course on participatory archives at RISD, and was a 2025 RISD Maharam Fellow. Previously at Indiana University, Avrie was a Cox Scholar, a design fellow with the Kinsey Institute, and has earned awards from the art history and graphic design departments.

She is currently taking freelance commissions and developing a publication based on her MFA thesis, countersense. You can email her at avrie.world at gmail dot com.

countersense

countersense embodies a counterhegemonic practice of sense-making; it is a practice of opening up our present conditions for critical reflection and remaking. Viewing graphic design as an interdisciplinary and expansive field of meaning-making, countersense is the framework I perform between perception and meaning-making, self and material conditions—always foregrounding what politics are chosen as a result of the personal.
Girlhood Arrested

Girlhood Arrested, is a reflection on my Girlhood Zine Workshop, dualities of carcerality-punishment and play-resistance, the possibilities of girlhood as an organizing construct of self outside of incarceration, and is a transhistorical engagement with the archival gaps of women's lives and stories.

Collaboration with Dr. Kennedi Johnson and the Visiting Room Project
everyday utopia

everyday utopia is a jacquard-woven blanket inspired by A Decolonial Feminism by François Vergès. Yik Heng Lee (RISD Textiles MFA ’26) interpreted the digital design by Jacob Marcus (RISD GD MFA '27) and me, indexing graphic pixels into woven structures and form.
RISD Graphic Design Triennial 2024

RISD GD Triennial 2024 website I hand-coded and designed with support from members of the web team.
RISD Graduate Studies

The custom letterforms were created from a library of forms gathered from RISD graduate studio and common spaces, like Fleet Library.
The End of History–History of the End

A book of two timelines. Section 1 (white pages), The End of History, documents key events contributing to the global dominance of neoliberalism. Section 2 (black pages), History of the End, documents global acts of resistance to it—highlighting that future alternatives have never been foreclosed.
On Measurement

How a culture views the body is historically and politically specific. Albrecht Dürer’s Instructions on Measurement, published in 1528, marks a transition of how the western body is represented—shifting from a feudal metaphysical understanding to a rationalized capitalist subject. On Measurement situates its representation within a lineage of technologies from 1528–today that aim to capture, measure, and analyze the body.
The houses, the air, the streets

Silk organza fabric prints with text from Karen Brodine's poem, June 78. The prints can be viewed together as a book—bound through grommets with binder rings—taken apart and viewed individually, or rearranged and clipped together horizontally—creating a modular, generative writing.
Activating Networks, Gathering Knowledge

This course investigates how web-based archives can be used to gather inspiration and conduct research for creative practices. Taught wintersession 2026 in RISD Graphic Design department.
Kinsey Institute

Misc. graphics from my time as graphic designer at the Kinsey Institute.
fragments of a future

A poster arranged by hand, using June, 78 by Karen Brodine as source material. The last stanza of the poem about envisioning and sensing a liberatory future, in which we and our communities are actually well is fragmented and rearranged into a new text.
Girlhood Zine Workshop

Dr. Kennedi Johnson and I conducted a zine workshop with formerly incarcerated narrators (including women and one trans masculine person) from the Visiting Room Project, a community archive in New Orleans, to reflect on girlhood as a tool for identity-construction outside of their experience with incarceration. The planning process was participatory: Kennedi and I held Zoom-meetings leading up to the workshop to introduce participants to zines and ask what narrators wanted to learn and make. The workshop and zines produced were featured in an offsite panel at the American Anthropological Association Conference 2025, Memory Work at the John Thompson Legacy Center, which was co-sponsored by the Society for the Anthropology of North America and the association of Black Anthropologists.
RISD Campus Exhibitions Gallery Brochure

Know-it-Yourself

A book about and for knowing through doing, instructing the reader to take the book apart, plant native seeds, and sew a pair of gardening gloves.

Collaboration with Jacob Marcus
An Empty Pedestal is an Invitation

Following protests and repeated vandalism of a Christopher Columbus statue in 2020, the city of Providence removed the statue from its pedestal in the Elmwood neighborhood. Zines and small graphics were distributed around Elmwood, providing this history and inviting residents to submit to a digital archive. Additionally, two 1:1 scale posters of the statue and the number of distributed zines conceptualize that participatory gestures can be even bigger than the monumental through their anti-monumental qualities.
luddite.com/munity

luddite.com/munity, a collaborative installation made with Jacob Marcus, speculates on the effects artificial intelligence will have on our lived experience: our interpersonal relationships, language, labor, cognition, and culture. Set in a fictional post-Providence in 2073, it imagines the struggle of a society facing the effects of climate change but stunted by their over-reliance on artificial intelligence.
